BUSINESS REQUIREMENTS
System Overview
The healthcare sector faces significant challenges in managing patient medical records across multiple
providers, ensuring data security, and providing authorized access to complete patient information.
Medical records are fragmented across different facilities, leading to incomplete histories, duplicate
tests, medication errors, and compromised care quality. Patients lack control over who accesses their
sensitive health information, and healthcare providers struggle with verifying record authenticity and
detecting unauthorized modifications.
MediChain addresses these challenges by providing a comprehensive healthcare data management
platform that logically centralizes patient medical records through a unified relational database while
supporting controlled, consent-based access across institutions. The system ensures data integrity
through tamper-evident mechanisms and gives patients complete control over access to their health
information. It maintains detailed records of all patient-provider interactions, tracks medications with
automated safety checks, manages laboratory test orders and results, implements intelligent consent
mechanisms, provides emergency access protocols for critical care, and maintains an immutable audit
trail with tamper detection capabilities.
We chose this domain because healthcare systems highlight the importance of data integrity, access
control, and auditing, which aligns strongly with the core objectives of this DBMS course.
Project Scope
The scope of MediChain for this project is limited to the design and implementation of a databasecentric healthcare information system, with emphasis on data modeling, integrity constraints, access
control, and auditability. This task focuses on high-level business requirements and scope definition;
detailed schema design, SQL queries, triggers, and transactions will be formally addressed in
subsequent tasks.
In-Scope
Patient registration and unique health ID management
Clinical encounters including outpatient, inpatient, and emergency visits
Prescription and medication tracking with automated safety checks
Laboratory test ordering and result management
Patient consent mechanisms and emergency access logging
Comprehensive audit logging of data access and modifications
Role-based access control for patients, doctors, nurses, pharmacists, and administrators
Out-of-Scope

schema design, integrity constraints, transactions, and access control rather than external system
integration or advanced analytics.
Patient Registration and Management
When an individual first seeks medical care at any participating healthcare facility, they must be
registered in the MediChain system. Each patient is assigned a government-issued health identification
number that serves as their unique identifier across all facilities, preventing duplicate registrations.
The registration captures the patient's complete name, date of birth, gender, blood group if known, and
multiple contact phone numbers. The system records residential address including street, city, state,
and postal code, with support for multiple addresses designated as primary residence, secondary
residence, or work address.
Emergency contact information includes the name, relationship to patient, contact phone number, and
address of persons to be notified in medical emergencies. The system maintains insurance policy
details including provider name, policy number, policy holder name if different from the patient,
coverage dates, and policy type. A patient may have multiple active insurance policies simultaneously,
with the system tracking each policy's validity period.
The system automatically calculates patient age based on date of birth for age-appropriate medical
protocols, vaccination eligibility, medication dosing, and statistical categorization into infants, children,
teenagers, adults, and senior citizens. All patient-related information will be stored in normalized
relational tables with appropriate primary keys and integrity constraints to avoid duplication and
maintain consistency.
Healthcare Provider and Hospital Management
Healthcare facilities register by providing hospital name, complete address, hospital license number,
contact information, facility type, and bed capacity. Facility types include general hospital, specialty
hospital, teaching hospital, trauma center, and primary health center. Medical practitioners register
with their medical license number, personal information, contact details, and area of specialization
including internal medicine, cardiology, pediatrics, orthopedics, neurology, oncology, and emergency
medicine. A doctor may have multiple specializations and practice at multiple hospitals. This many-tomany association between doctors and hospitals will be explicitly modeled in the database using
relationship tables.
Healthcare facilities organize staff into clinical departments such as emergency medicine, cardiology,
orthopedics, pediatrics, surgery, radiology, pathology, and pharmacy. Each department belongs to a
specific hospital, and staff members are assigned to appropriate departments based on their role and
specialization.
Clinical Encounter Documentation
Every patient visit is documented as a clinical encounter. When a patient arrives, staff creates an
encounter record by selecting the patient, recording the visit date and time, assigning the treating
doctor, and specifying the encounter type. Types include outpatient consultation, inpatient admission,
emergency visit, and follow-up visit. For inpatient encounters, the system records admission date and
time, assigned bed number, and eventual discharge date.
During the encounter, nursing staff records vital signs including systolic and diastolic blood pressure,
pulse rate, body temperature, respiratory rate, height, weight, and oxygen saturation. All measurements
are timestamped for trend analysis. The doctor documents the chief complaint, performs systematic
examination, and records clinical notes. Each encounter serves as a central entity linking patients,
doctors, diagnoses, procedures, prescriptions, and laboratory orders.
The doctor establishes diagnoses using standardized ICD-10 diagnosis codes. Multiple diagnoses can be
recorded, classified as primary or secondary, with status indicating provisional, confirmed, or ruled out.
Medical procedures are documented using CPT procedure codes, including procedure date, performing
doctor, procedural notes, and any complications. The doctor determines follow-up needs and
documents the treatment plan.
Prescription Management and Medication Safety
When medication is needed, the doctor creates a prescription by selecting the drug from a
comprehensive catalog containing generic names, brand names, drug classes, dosage forms, and
manufacturer information. The prescription specifies medication, dosage strength, dosage form,
frequency of administration, route, duration of treatment, and total quantity to be dispensed. The
doctor provides patient instructions including whether to take with food, activities to avoid, and what to
do if a dose is missed.
Before finalizing any prescription, the system performs critical safety checks. It verifies that the patient
is not allergic to the prescribed medication by checking allergy records. Patient allergy information
includes the allergen, reaction description, severity classification, identification date, and current
status. If a severe or life-threatening allergy is detected, the system prevents prescription completion
unless the doctor provides explicit override with documented justification.
The system performs comprehensive drug interaction checking by examining all active patient
medications against a database of known interactions categorized by severity as major, moderate, or
minor. When a potential interaction is detected, the system generates an alert displaying both
medications, the interaction nature, severity, and clinical recommendations. For major severity
interactions, the system requires physician acknowledgment and clinical reasoning documentation
before proceeding, with all override decisions permanently logged. These safety rules will later be
enforced using database constraints and triggers to ensure correctness even under concurrent access.
Prescriptions progress through status states including active, completed, and discontinued.
Pharmacists retrieve prescriptions, verify patient identity, confirm validity, dispense medication, and
record the dispensing transaction, creating a complete medication history.
Laboratory Testing and Diagnostic Services
When diagnostic testing is needed, doctors create laboratory orders by selecting the patient, choosing
tests from the catalog, indicating priority level as routine, urgent, or stat, and providing relevant clinical
information. The laboratory test catalog contains test codes, descriptive names, specimen types
required, collection procedures, and turnaround times.
When orders are placed, laboratory technicians record specimen collection details including collection
date, container types, and specimen identifiers for tracking. Each specimen receives a unique identifier
ensuring proper attribution of results. Laboratory order creation, specimen collection, and result entry
will be treated as transactional operations to maintain consistency across related tables.
Laboratory personnel perform tests and enter results. For quantitative tests, results include measured
value, unit of measurement, and reference range. For qualitative tests, predefined result options are
selected. The system automatically compares numeric results against reference ranges which vary by
patient age and gender, flagging abnormal results. Critical results indicating dangerous conditions are
specially flagged and generate immediate alerts requiring physician acknowledgment. Results undergo
verification by laboratory directors before release to ordering physicians.
Patient Consent and Access Authorization
A fundamental principle of MediChain is that patients own their medical information and control who
accesses it. When patients wish to grant healthcare providers access, they create consent records
specifying the provider, level of access, purpose, and temporal restrictions. Access levels include full
access to all records, partial access restricted to specific record types, or emergency-only access.
Purposes include ongoing treatment, consultation, insurance claim processing, research participation,
or legal purposes. Patients can specify effective dates and optional expiration dates.
All consent decisions are recorded with complete details including patient, provider, access level,
purpose, effective date, expiration date, and creation timestamp. Patients can view all active consents
and revoke consent at any time, with revocation immediately effective. The system enforces consent
policies by verifying active consent exists before allowing record access, checking expiration status,
confirming requested information type is covered, and validating access purpose matches consent
purpose.
Emergency Access and Critical Care Override
Medical emergencies create situations where immediate record access is necessary without prior
consent. The system implements a controlled emergency access mechanism allowing healthcare
providers to override consent restrictions in true emergencies while maintaining accountability. When a
doctor invokes emergency access, they specify the emergency type, provide detailed justification, and
acknowledge that access will be logged and reviewed.
Upon invoking emergency access, the system grants immediate access to critical medical information
including current medications, allergies, chronic conditions, and recent laboratory results. Every
emergency access is permanently logged with patient identification, accessing provider, timestamp,
emergency type, justification, specific records viewed, and duration. Following each emergency access,
the system schedules compliance committee review to examine whether access was justified and
appropriate. Patients are notified after emergency access occurs, receiving information about when
their records were accessed, by whom, for what emergency, and how to review details or file
complaints.
Comprehensive Audit Trail and Data Integrity Assurance
To ensure accountability, detect unauthorized access, support regulatory compliance, and provide
transparency, MediChain maintains meticulous logs of virtually every system action. User login
attempts are recorded with date, time, username, IP address, device type, and success status. Every
patient record access is logged with patient identification, specific records viewed, accessing user,
authorizing consent, stated purpose, timestamp, and view duration.
When medical records are created, modified, or deleted, these operations are logged capturing record
type, before and after values for modifications, performing user, timestamp, workstation, and
explanatory notes. This creates a complete version history essential for quality assurance, medicolegal
purposes, and detecting erroneous or fraudulent data entry.
To prevent tampering, audit logs are protected using tamper-evident mechanisms implemented at the
database level, allowing detection of unauthorized modifications. If anyone attempts to modify or
delete an audit log entry, this tampering will break the verification chain and become detectable,
providing strong assurance that the audit trail represents a true and unaltered record. The system can
periodically verify audit chain integrity, immediately detecting discrepancies. When tampering is
detected, security administrators are alerted to investigate.
The audit log is designed to be immutable, meaning entries cannot be altered or removed through any
normal system operation. Even users with administrative privileges cannot modify audit log entries
without triggering tampering detection. This logical immutability is enforced through database triggers
and constraints that intercept modification attempts and either block operations or generate highpriority security alerts. The combination of tamper-evident verification and immutability enforcement
ensures the audit trail serves as a trustworthy and legally defensible record of all system activities.

Database-Centric View of the System
From a DBMS perspective, the system consists of core entities such as Patient, Hospital, Doctor,
Encounter, Prescription, Medication, LaboratoryOrder, LabResult, Consent, and AuditLog. The design
involves multiple one-to-many and many-to-many relationships, strong referential integrity constraints,
and rule enforcement through constraints and triggers. Subsequent project tasks will focus on ER
modeling, relational schema design, SQL query implementation, triggers, and transaction analysis.
In subsequent project tasks, these entities and relationships will be formally represented using an
Entity-Relationship diagram, converted into a normalized relational schema, and implemented using
SQL Data Definition Language. Business rules described above will be enforced using primary keys,
foreign keys, check constraints, triggers, and transactional logic. Sample SQL queries, stored
procedures, and transaction scenarios will demonstrate correctness, concurrency handling, and
integrity preservation.
Business Rules and Operational Constraints
The MediChain system operates according to business rules ensuring data accuracy, clinical safety,
proper authorization, and operational consistency. Each patient must have a unique health
identification number. Patient age calculated from date of birth must be consistent with the recorded
birth date. Every prescription must be associated with exactly one patient and one prescribing doctor.
Laboratory test results must link to valid laboratory orders. Consent records must clearly identify both
patient and provider.
Temporal consistency rules ensure logical date ordering. Encounter dates cannot be in the future. A
patient's date of birth must be earlier than all encounters. Prescription start dates must precede end
dates. Consent effective dates must precede expiration dates. Laboratory result dates must follow
specimen collection dates.
Clinical safety rules prevent dangerous medical errors. Patients must not be prescribed medications to
which they have severe allergies unless the doctor provides explicit override with justification. Major
drug interactions must trigger alerts requiring physician acknowledgment. Critical laboratory results
must be acknowledged by ordering physicians within defined timeframes. Emergency access must
always be logged with justification and reviewed by compliance personnel.
Authorization and access control rules enforce proper data protection. Users can only access records
with explicit authorization through active consent, emergency access, or assigned clinical role. Consent
revocations take effect immediately. Audit log entries cannot be modified or deleted by any user.
Patients have the right to view all audit log entries documenting access to their records.
Data quality rules ensure accuracy and completeness. Mandatory data fields must be populated before
records can be saved. All diagnosis codes must be valid ICD-10 codes from the current master list. All
procedure codes must be valid CPT codes. Laboratory result values must be numeric for quantitative
tests and match predefined options for qualitative tests. Contact information must conform to standard
format requirements.
Many of these rules will be enforced using database-level mechanisms such as foreign key constraints,
CHECK constraints, triggers, and transaction isolation to ensure data correctness and consistency even
under concurrent access conditions.
ASSUMPTIONS AND LIMITATIONS
The system assumes that all participating hospitals, laboratories, and pharmacies are registered within
the MediChain platform and comply with standardized data entry protocols. The project focuses on
logical database design and correctness rather than real-time system performance at national scale.
Security mechanisms such as encryption and authentication are considered at a conceptual level, with
emphasis on database-level enforcement through constraints, triggers, and auditing.
